Chapter one

She booked because she wanted herself back.

Katherine drove a few hours to the studio last November. By the time she sat down on my couch six months later to answer questions on camera, the woman across from me wasn't the same one who'd made the booking. But she remembered exactly why she did.

In her words

"I wanted to feel confident. I wanted to gain some of this confidence back that I've lost. Through years of feeling less than because I don't look a certain way."

— Katherine

The reason most women book this isn't the photos. The photos are the artifact. The reason is something underneath that — usually some version of I want to feel like myself again. I want to stop apologizing for what I look like. I want to know I can do something hard.

In her words

"I grew up in a family where my cousins and my sister looked a certain way, and I was kind of ostracized — made the black sheep — because I didn't look like them."

— Katherine

She'd carried that for years. Not visible to most people. Visible to her every morning.

In her words

"I think what stops a lot of us from doing anything in life is overthinking what could go wrong, instead of thinking — what if it goes right?"

— Katherine
Chapter two

She did her homework.

Katherine doesn't make impulse decisions about anything. Her dad was in law enforcement. The phrase she grew up with was "the devil's in the details." So before she sent a deposit, she dug.

In her words

"I loved the diversity — but not just the diversity of who you photographed. The diversity of shape. The modeling industry is very subjective to a certain look. I'm more comfortable working with someone who's worked with everybody."

— Katherine

That part matters. When you scroll a photographer's gallery and only see one body type, one age, one skin tone, one polished version of beauty — your brain says this isn't for me. When you see every figure, every age, every season of life represented well — your brain says maybe I could be in there too.

In her words

"Not everybody is who they are on the internet. I was like, you know what? I'm gonna do my research so at least I'm covered."

— Katherine

She dug into reviews. She read what past clients said. She told her dad. (Her dad wanted to run a background check.) And then she booked.

In her words

"I was a little nervous working with a man, if I'm gonna be honest. But at the end of the day, you're an artist. Your medium happens to be boudoir. I wasn't going to judge a book by its cover."

— Katherine
Chapter three

The day was awkward — for about twenty minutes.

Every woman who walks into the studio for the first time has the same nervous-system response. Body says: this is unfamiliar, what am I doing. Fight or flight. Katherine had it too.

In her words

"When it started, I was a full-on 8.5, 9. I felt so awkward, because even in swimsuits I felt awkward. So this was the first time I was glammed up in a lingerie set, and I'm just awkward in general."

— Katherine, on a 1-to-10 awkwardness scale

My job in the first 15 minutes is the same job every time: pose every shot. Head, shoulders, hands, hips, breath. You don't have to figure out what to do. The instruction never stops.

In her words

"You walked me through each pose, each step — where to place my hands, where to turn my head. People who don't know what they're doing want to be told step by step."

— Katherine

The other thing — the unglamorous-but-essential thing — is that the room is loud with affirmation. Not creepy. Just verbal. Yes, this is looking good. You're doing great. Lift your chin a quarter inch. Perfect. Most women have never been spoken to that way, and certainly not while they're dressed like that.

In her words

"You were definitely my hype man the entire time. Kayla too. Just hearing it confirmed verbally — yes, this is looking good, you're doing great — for someone like me who's never done it, that 'attagirl' was huge."

— Katherine

By the third look she was laughing. By the end of the day she had to be reminded to stop posing.

In her words

"By the end, I was probably a 2 or 3. It was just awkward because of the poses, not what I was wearing. We were both like, it's fine, we'll figure it out."

— Katherine

A few hours in. The 8.5 became a 2.

Chapter four

Then she saw herself.

Same-day reveal means we shoot, we eat lunch while I edit, and then the gallery comes up on a 75-inch screen. There's no waiting weeks for a download link. The work you did that morning — you see it that afternoon. The room goes quiet.

In her words

"I wanted to leave the room. I wanted to run down the street. Because it's the big screen, and it's in your face."

— Katherine

That reaction is normal. Almost universal, actually. The first 10 photos are uncomfortable to watch yourself in because you're looking with the eyes you've used for forty years. By photo 30 or 40, those eyes start to soften.

In her words

"I saw a glamorized version of myself. It was overwhelming, but in a positive way. It was like being bombarded with what others see — and what you don't see."

— Katherine
In her words

"The silhouette was mine. The face was mine. There were lumps and bumps that were mine. But it was shot in such a tasteful, artistic way. I remember being so uncomfortable in those poses — but in the photos, I don't look like it."

— Katherine

She'd told me at booking she expected to like one or two images. She left having chosen many more than that.

In her words

"I thought I was only going to get, like, a picture or two. I did not think I would fall in love with more than one."

— Katherine
Chapter five

Six months later, here's what actually changed.

Katherine came back six months after her session to answer every buyer-question on camera. The thing I noticed most wasn't what she said. It was how she sat. Posture different. Shoulders different. Voice different. I'm not making that up — go watch the footage.

In her words

"It felt like releasing something I'd kept in a box on a shelf for over a decade. All my insecurities. All my self-confidence issues. All my self-doubt. I think it was finally set free."

— Katherine
In her words

"I'm not cured. You're great, but I don't think you cured all of it. But it felt like a stepping stone to finally being comfortable with who I am right now."

— Katherine

She framed the realization in a way I've heard from dozens of past clients. The realization is rarely "I'm beautiful now". It's almost always "I'm allowed to stop waiting."

In her words

"If I don't love myself now, I don't think I'm going to love myself anytime — even if I'm 100 pounds lighter, 100 pounds heavier."

— Katherine
In her words

"It gave me the confidence to try dating again. So… things have changed."

— Katherine
