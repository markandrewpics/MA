her story

"It Felt Like Releasing Something I'd Kept in a Box for a Decade."

A past client opens up about why she booked, what the shoot actually felt like, and why she'd tell a friend to do it.

Catherine drove a few hours to the studio last November to do something she'd never done: walk in, put on lingerie, and stand in front of a camera. She was 26. Her confidence, in her words, was "really low." She had never bought lingerie before. She had never had her hair and makeup professionally done. Six months later, she sat down with me to tell the truth about how it actually went.

Why Did Catherine Book a Boudoir Shoot?

Catherine booked her boudoir shoot to gain back confidence she felt she had lost over years of comparing herself to other women. She was 26, in a season of life that felt like a hamster wheel, and she wanted to do something that pushed her outside the mundane and reminded her she could be sexy, confident, and proud of who she is.

She first saw an ad for the studio scrolling Facebook one night. The pull was simple: she wanted to feel different about herself. She'd grown up the "black sheep" in a family where her cousins and sister looked a certain way and she didn't.

"I had always admired women who would do these boudoir shoots. They looked so confident, so calm — like they weren't stressed at all. And I was like, that's me. I need something to help me loosen up."

— Catherine

The decision to actually book came down to one mental shift, and it's the line I haven't been able to stop thinking about since:

"I think what stops a lot of us from doing anything in life is overthinking what could go wrong, instead of thinking — what if it goes right?"

— Catherine

What Made Her Choose This Studio?

Catherine looked at multiple boudoir photographers before booking. What sold her was the diversity in the portfolio — different body types, ages, and shapes — and the artistic edge she didn't see in other photographers. She also read reviews from past clients who described feeling nervous and ending up comfortable.

"I loved the diversity — but not just the diversity of who you photographed. The diversity of shape. The modeling industry is very subjective to a certain look. I'm more comfortable working with someone who's worked with everybody."

— Catherine

She'll also tell you straight: she was a little nervous about working with a male photographer.

"I was a little nervous, if I'm gonna be honest. But at the end of the day, you're an artist. Your medium happens to be boudoir. I wasn't going to judge a book by its cover."

— Catherine

The reviews from other women — describing the same nerves she had — were the tipping point.

How Did Her Family React When She Told Them?

Catherine told her family before her shoot, mostly for safety. Her dad — who works in law enforcement — was protective and skeptical at first. Ultimately, the takeaway from her parents was simple: you're an adult, you can do what you want, just be safe.

Family conversations are one of the quiet hesitations almost every woman wrestles with before booking. Catherine handled it the same way most of my clients do — she told the people she trusted, didn't ask for permission, and went forward.

"They were a little hesitant — that's just who they are. But they said, 'You're an adult, you can do whatever.' I wasn't waiting for approval. But I told them, just for safety."

— Catherine

And a line I want to repeat for any woman who's let what their family might think stop her from booking:

"A photo does not change who you are as a person. I've seen women wear less in swimsuits."

— Catherine

How Awkward Did the Shoot Actually Feel?

On a scale of 1 to 10, Catherine started the shoot at an 8.5 to 9 on awkwardness. By the middle, she was at a 6. By the end, she was a 2 or 3 — laughing through poses on an air mattress in her last set. The drop happened, she said, because she stopped overthinking and started trusting the process.

"When it started, I was a full-on 8.5, 9. Even in swimsuits I felt awkward. So this was the first time I was glammed up in lingerie, and I just felt awkward. I'm already here. I can't leave."

— Catherine

Two things, in her words, helped pull her out of her head:

Being walked through every pose, step by step.

"You walked me through each pose, each step — where to look, how to place my hands, how to turn my head. People who don't know what they're doing want to be told step by step."

— Catherine

Being hyped up the entire time.

"You were definitely my hype man the entire time. Kayla too. Just hearing it confirmed verbally — yes, this is looking good, you're doing great — for someone like me who's never done it, that 'attagirl' was huge."

— Catherine

By the time she got to her last outfit — the most revealing one of the day — she said the awkwardness was gone:

"It was laughable at that point. We were both like, it's fine, we'll figure it out."

— Catherine

How Did She Figure Out What to Wear?

Catherine had never bought lingerie in her life before the shoot. She picked her outfits by stepping outside her comfort zone — moving away from black, leaning into color and texture, and choosing pieces that felt like her, even if she'd never worn anything like them before.

"I'm more comfortable wearing black because I was told from a young age that it's slimming. But in lingerie, that doesn't really work. I tried to stick with colors I love — I love green, because I have hazel eyes. So I found this great olive-green bustier."

— Catherine

She also gravitated toward lace for "that vintage vibe." And she gave herself permission to pick pieces she'd never wear in public:

"Comfortable not as in I can walk around town in this — but comfortable as in I can do a shoot in it, and I'm fine."

— Catherine

What Was the Photo Reveal Like?

Walking back into the studio after lunch to see her images for the first time, Catherine wanted to run out the door. But the moment her photos came up on the screen, she didn't see what she expected. She saw what she described as a "glamorized version of myself" — and for the first time, looking at her own image was something other than uncomfortable.

"I wanted to leave the room. I wanted to run down the street. Because it's the big screen, and it's in your face."

— Catherine

Then her photos came up.

"I saw a glamorized version of myself. It was overwhelming, but in a positive way. It was like being bombarded with what others see — and what you don't see."

— Catherine

The thing that stayed with her wasn't the editing. It was that she could still see herself in the images.

"The silhouette was mine. The face was mine. There were lumps and bumps that were mine. But it was shot in such a tasteful, artistic way. I remember being so uncomfortable in those poses — but in the photos, I don't look like it."

— Catherine

She also said something I want every woman who's nervous about a shoot to hear:

"I'm not ashamed of doing this. I'm not ashamed of what I wore. At the end of the day, it's me. Just me in less clothes — but still me."

— Catherine

Was the Investment Worth It?

Catherine went into her shoot expecting to walk away with one or two images. She left with a full package — album, wall art, digital files — through a monthly payment plan. It was more than she'd planned to spend, and she has zero regrets.

"Was it more than I was expecting to pay? Yes. But I now have something incredible that will last for an eternity. I'm making monthly payments. It is fine."

— Catherine

For her, the math worked out the moment she saw her photos:

"I did not think I would fall in love with more than one. I'm happy I did it. I'm not going to regret it."

— Catherine

What Changed After the Shoot?

What surprised Catherine most wasn't the photos. It was what came after. She described the experience as cathartic — as if something she had been holding on to since she was prepubescent had finally been released. She isn't "cured" of self-doubt, in her words. But she has the confidence now to do things she wouldn't have done before — including starting to date again.

"It felt like releasing something I'd kept in a box on a shelf for over a decade. All my insecurities. All my self-confidence issues. All my self-doubt. I think it was finally set free."

— Catherine

She still looks at her images dozens of times. She's shown people. Some have been shocked — but, she's quick to add, not in a bad way.

"I was finally starting to feel, even days later, that I'm not ashamed."

— Catherine

And then this:

"It gave me the confidence to try dating again. So… things have changed."

— Catherine

What Would Catherine Tell a Woman Who's Still on the Fence?

Catherine's message to any woman thinking about booking — but stuck in the same overthinking she was stuck in — is simple. Do it for yourself. Not your partner. Not anyone else. You.

"You're more likely to be proud that you did it than regret it. Because at the end of the day, it's your life. It's who you are. And it does not change who you are if you want to do a risqué photo or two."

— Catherine

She left me with a line I've thought about every day since the interview:

in her words

"It's a hug to yourself. It's not a punch."

"Self-criticizing who you are is really debilitating. This was very helpful in recovering from being your own bully."

If Something in Her Story Felt Familiar

Most of the women who walk into the studio sound a lot like Catherine on her first call. Nervous. Quietly hoping it'll be different. A little tired of being their own bully. If any part of her story felt like it was describing you, that's the signal.

You don't have to be ready. You don't have to have it figured out. Catherine wasn't either. She just decided that the version of her life where she stayed scared was no longer the version she wanted.
